## Case 1: Why Is Premium Tea Hard to Sell? (Real Case)

### Background

A tea brand positioned as premium, priced at $2,000 RMB per kilogram. The quality was genuinely excellent—the founder was a national intangible cultural heritage inheritor. But sales were struggling, with consumers saying "it's too expensive." The owner considered lowering the price.

### Analysis Process

**Step 1: Evaluate Product Strength**
- The craftsmanship and raw materials were genuinely better than competitors at the same price point
- But product strength alone couldn't justify the purchase to consumers

**Step 2: Profile the Consumer**
- People buying $2,000 tea primarily use it for **gift-giving**
- Gift recipients: Tea connoisseurs, regular tea drinkers
- How recipients use it: Entertaining guests, social situations

**Step 3: Discover the True Motivation**
- Here, tea functions as **social currency**
- Origin, story—these are baseline requirements. Connoisseurs already know this. Not a differentiator.

**Step 4: Find the Differentiation Direction**
- Real differentiation lies in "feeling"—**cultural attributes**
- Elevate from product attributes (chestnut aroma, floral notes) to cultural attributes ("Springtime at West Lake")
- Reduce tea elements in packaging, emphasize cultural symbols (Su Shi, Qianlong Emperor, West Lake)
- Create small scenario stories in marketing—not seeking truth, but stories worth telling

### Core Insight

Consumers aren't buying tea. They're buying **"I have a story to tell"**—the story is the added value of social currency.

### Marketing Recommendations

1. Packaging: Shift from "tea" to "cultural symbol"
2. Communication: Create small scenario stories with celebrity appeal
3. Don't lower prices—build purchase reasons
4. The consumer base stays the same, but they want higher social currency added value when choosing

## Case 2: Why Do Children's Education Products Have Low Repurchase Rates? (Real Case)

### Background

A children's education brand with solid course quality and positive parent feedback. But repurchase rates were very low—parents signed up for one semester and didn't renew.

### Analysis Process

**Step 1: Identify Value Misalignment**
- The problem the course solves ≠ The problem parents are willing to pay for continuously
- When parents buy educational products, they're fundamentally solving **their own anxiety**

**Step 2: Understand the Repurchase Logic**
- Repurchase requires a **continuous anxiety cycle**—buy to solve, stop buying and anxiety returns
- Good educational products should continuously generate anxiety

**Step 3: Find the Differentiation Direction**
- Everyone else is creating achievement anxiety—competition is fierce
- **Different approach**: Point to future anxiety—What qualities will children need as adults?

**Step 4: Identify Parent Pain Points**
- Parents' current pain points are what they don't want their children to experience
- Anxiety of middle managers aged 35-45:
  - AI replacement, outdated skills, squeezed workplace value
  - Academic competition is meaningless—AI already crushes human information processing
  - What should really be cultivated: **Cross-information domain associative thinking**—an ability AI cannot replicate

### Core Insight

Educational products shouldn't compete on academic achievement. They should cultivate **uniquely human, AI-proof abilities**.

### Marketing Recommendations

1. Exit the achievement anxiety赛道 (market segment)—competition is too fierce
2. Point to the future: Abilities children need in the AI era as adults
3. Mine parents' existing pain points and convert them into educational anxiety
4. Cultivate "cross-information domain associative thinking" ability

## Case 3: Why Do Parents Buy Expensive Educational Products?

### Background

An online education product costing $20,000-30,000 RMB annually. Parents grit their teeth and buy.

### Deep Insights

**True Motivations**:
1. **Anxiety Relief** — "At least I tried my best"
2. **Security** — Everyone else is buying; what if I don't?
3. **Self-Justification** — I spent the money, so it's not my responsibility anymore

Parents aren't buying education. They're buying **anxiety relief**.

### Marketing Recommendations
Don't just emphasize results. Emphasize:
- Anxiety resonance ("You're worried about... right?")
- Responsibility transfer ("Leave it to us, you can relax")
- Social proof ("Parents just like you are using this")

## Case 6: How Do Enterprises Choose CRM Systems? (B2B Decision Case)

### Background

A mid-sized manufacturing company (500 employees) planning to purchase a CRM system with a budget of 500,000 RMB.

**Candidate Solutions:**
- Solution A: Well-known major vendor, standardized product, transparent pricing
- Solution B: Startup, supports deep customization, slightly higher price

### Core Logic of B2B Decisions

**⚠️ B2B and B2C are completely different: B2C analyzes personal psychological motivations; B2B analyzes enterprise stage and goals.**

#### Step 1: Determine the Enterprise Stage

| Stage | Characteristics | Core Goal |
|-------|----------------|-----------|
| **Startup** | High survival pressure, limited resources | Survival/Growth |
| **Growth** | Rapid business expansion, team scaling | Growth/Scaling |
| **Stability** | Stable customers, stable performance | Cost reduction, efficiency |
| **Struggling** | Stalled growth, facing challenges | Breakthrough/Transformation |
| **Transformation** | Business model changing | New growth points |

#### Step 2: Identify Pain Point Types

- **Growth Pain Points**: Difficult acquisition, low conversion, insufficient new business
- **Efficiency Pain Points**: Chaotic processes, scattered data, high management costs
- **Transformation Pain Points**: Old model failing, need new capabilities

#### Step 3: Match Product Needs

| Pain Point Type | Product Need | Matching Solution |
|----------------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Growth pain** | Customization, flexibility, deep exploration | Small company solution |
| **Efficiency pain** | Standardization, stability, low cost | Major vendor solution |
| **Transformation pain** | High customization, rapid iteration | Small company solution |

### Comparing Two Scenarios

#### Scenario 1: Stable-Stage Enterprise

**Current Situation:**
- Stable customer base, stable sales performance
- Scattered data, high management costs

**Need Analysis:**
- Core goal: Cost reduction, efficiency
- Pain point: High data management costs (not a growth problem)
- Product need: Standardization, stability, low price

**Decision: Major Vendor CRM**
- Standardized product just meets needs
- Customization features not needed
- Major vendor is more stable, lower risk

#### Scenario 2: Startup/Struggling Enterprise

**Current Situation:**
- Customer growth stagnating, insufficient mining of existing customers
- Need to find incremental opportunities from existing data

**Need Analysis:**
- Core goal: Growth/Breakthrough
- Pain point: Insufficient new business, need deep mining
- Product need: Customization, flexibility, deep support

**Decision: Small Company CRM**
- Need customized solutions for specific problems
- Major vendor standardized products can't meet needs
- Small company willing to deeply cooperate and quickly adjust

### B2B Analysis Framework Summary

```
Enterprise Stage → Core Goal → Pain Point Type → Product Need → Solution Match
```

**Key Principles:**
1. B2B decisions are "collective decisions," but the core is **enterprise goals**, not personal psychology
2. Enterprises at different stages have completely different needs for the same product
3. Product selection depends on "fit," not "good or bad"
4. Personal psychological motivations are secondary factors; enterprise goals are the primary decision driver

### Role of Individual Decision-Makers in B2B

While B2B core is enterprise goals, individual decision-makers still have psychological motivations:

| Role | Psychological Motivation | Decision Tendency |
|------|-------------------------|------------------|
| Initiator | Wants to achieve results, wants promotion | Push for change |
| Execution layer | Fears trouble, fears responsibility | Tends toward stable solutions |
| Decision layer | Focuses on ROI, risk | Balances cost and benefit |

**But these are secondary**—if the product doesn't match enterprise goals, personal tendencies get corrected.

## Case 7: How Do Coffee Brands Raise Prices? (Price Sensitivity Analysis)

### Background

A coffee brand, current price 25 RMB/cup, wants to raise to 35 RMB.

**Dilemma:**
- Limited space, cannot improve seating environment
- Direct price increase will lose existing customers

### Analysis Process

#### Step 1: Determine the Price Range

| Price Range | Consumer Type | Core Appeal |
|------------|---------------|-------------|
| **10-30 RMB** | Mass market | Value for money, taste, volume |
| **30+ RMB** | Elite/White collar | Consumption experience (composite value) |

**25 RMB → 35 RMB means:**
- Crossing from "mass consumption range" to "elite consumption range"
- Consumer appeal shifts from "the product itself" to "composite experience"

#### Step 2: Since Space Can't Upgrade, What Replacements?

**Composite experience composition:**
- Space environment ❌ Cannot change
- Packaging design ✓ Can upgrade
- Brand story ✓ Can create
- Product scarcity ✓ Can build

#### Step 3: How to Not Hurt Existing Users?

**Strategy: New series priced separately, detached from existing products**

- Old products: Continue at 25 RMB
- New series: 35 RMB, supported by story

### Solution

**Brand story construction:**

> "We explored over 85 coffee plantations worldwide and discovered a unique flavor in an obscure origin..."

**Execution points:**
1. New series has separate naming (e.g., "Flavor Discovery Series")
2. Use story to explain "why it's worth 35 RMB"
3. People willing to pay for stories → Choose 35 RMB
4. People who only care about value → Continue choosing 25 RMB
5. The two user groups don't interfere with each other

### Why Can Price Increases Be Accepted?

**Underlying logic:**

1. **Reasonableness of cost transfer**
   - Users understand you incurred costs (global search, obscure origin)
   - Transferring this cost to price is reasonable

2. **Novelty-seeking psychology**
   - Users are willing to pay premium for "new experience"
   - "Let me try" itself is a consumption motive

3. **Self-discovery incentive**
   - User discovers they like the new flavor → "Turns out I like this" → Sense of achievement
   - This achievement drives continuous repurchase

**Complete chain:**
```
Story (global flavor search) → Cost reasonableness → Novelty → Discover I like it → Self-discovery achievement → Continuous repurchase
```

### Newly Discovered Psychological Motivation: Self-Discovery

This differs from traditional psychological needs:

| Psychological Need | Typical Manifestation |
|------------------|----------------------|
| Security | Fear of wrong purchase, fear of regret |
| Superiority | Being better than others |
| Social Currency | Something to discuss, something to show |
| **Self-Discovery** | Discovering new aspects of self, gaining achievement |

**Consumption driven by "Self-Discovery":**
- Trying new brands, flavors, styles
- Discovering preferences through consumption
- Gaining achievement of "knowing myself better"

### Core Methodology

**Three-layer logic for price increases:**

```
What price range is the consumer in → Determines appeal type → Determines how to raise price
```

| Original Price Range | Target Price Range | Price Increase Method |
|---------------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 10-30 RMB → 10-30 RMB | Product upgrade (taste/volume) |
| 10-30 RMB → 30+ RMB | Story upgrade + New series detachment |
| 30+ RMB → Higher | Composite experience upgrade |

**Key Principles:**
1. Price increase ≠ Price increase; price increase = Providing more value
2. If neither product nor scenario changes, no price increase is acceptable
3. Price range determines consumer appeal
4. Crossing price critical points requires story and brand added value support
5. New series priced separately to protect existing users
6. Story must help users understand "cost reasonableness"
7. Stimulate "self-discovery" motivation to drive trial and repurchase

## Case 8: How Do Sports Shoes Raise Prices? (Function + Emotion Dual Drive)

### Background

A mass-market sports shoe brand, current price 300 RMB, wants to raise to 450 RMB (+50%).

User profile: 18-25 year-old students, young professionals starting their careers.

### Analysis: Dual Attributes of Sports Shoes

Sports shoes differ from coffee—they have two core attributes:

| Attribute | Consumer Appeal | Price Increase Path |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Equipment attribute** | Sports performance | Technology iteration |
| **Fashion attribute** | Good-looking appearance | Design upgrade |

### Price Increase Path 1: Technology Iteration

**Logic chain:**
```
Technology innovation → Sports performance improvement → Meets psychological expectations → Willing to pay more
```

**Key elements:**
- Users must "logically recognize" your technology
- Technology iteration must explain "why it helps me run faster/jump higher"
- Users have psychological expectations for sports performance; technology must match these expectations

**Real success cases:**

**Anta (Technology path success):**
- Strategy: Constantly pursue technological breakthroughs
- Case: Basketball shoes with forefoot cushioning technology (lattice printing)
- Result: Sold at 1,200-1,300 RMB, far exceeding other Anta product price points, outstanding success

**Li-Ning (Design path success):**
- Strategy: Fashion week releases + China Li-Ning design style
- Case: Paris Fashion Week, cutting-edge design, National Tide (Guochao) style
- Result: Brand premium significantly increased, became a trend symbol

**These two cases validate:**
- Technology path is viable: Anta proved tech innovation can support high prices
- Design path is viable: Li-Ning proved design upgrades can elevate brand premium

### Price Increase Path 2: Design Upgrade

**Logic chain:**
```
Design story → Psychological experience improvement → Identity recognition → Willing to pay more
```

**Key elements:**
- Design isn't just about looking good; it needs a story
- Stories create consumption experiences
- Fashion = Identity expression

**Cases:**
- AJ collabs: Basketball player stories + scarcity → Identity symbol
- Yeezy: Kanye design + street culture → Trendsetter identity

### Comparing Two Paths

| Path | Target User | Price Ceiling |
|------|------------|---------------|
| Technology iteration | Sports enthusiasts | Medium (function has a ceiling) |
| Design upgrade | Fashion-forward crowd | High (emotion has no ceiling) |

**Best strategy: Technology + Design dual drive**
- Technology provides "reasons to buy"
- Design provides "reasons to love"

### Core Methodology

**Category attributes determine price increase path:**

| Category Type | Core Attribute | Price Increase Path |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Consumables (coffee) | Experience-type | Story + Self-discovery |
| Equipment-type (sports shoes) | Function + Emotion | Technology + Design |
| Pure function-type (appliances) | Function | Technology iteration |
| Pure emotion-type (luxury) | Emotion | Story + Identity |

**Key Principles:**
1. Sports shoes have dual attributes; can choose one path or pursue dual drive
2. Technology path: Users must "logically recognize"
3. Design path: Not just looking good; needs a story
4. Technology provides purchase reasons; design provides emotional premium

## Case 9: How Do Appliances Raise Prices? (Life State Anchoring)

### Background

A robot vacuum brand, current price 2,000 RMB, wants to raise to 2,800 RMB (+40%).

User profile: 30-40 year-old married couples with children.

### Analysis: When to Use Technology Path vs. Life State Path?

**Key judgment standard: Tech price gap with competitors**

| Situation | Consumer Thinking | Strategy |
|----------|------------------|----------|
| Small tech gap | Tech comparison | Tech path storytelling |
| Large tech gap | Value trap | Break free from tech → Life state anchoring |

**Once trapped in value-for-money thinking:**
- Users constantly compare specs
- Your price increase justification gets questioned
- Very hard to convince users to pay more

**Solution: Break free from tech comparison, anchor to life state**

### What Is "Life State Anchoring"?

**Essence:**
- Not selling product features
- Selling "the life you want"
- Once anchored, users stop comparing competitor tech specs

**Robot vacuum example:**

| Path | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Tech path** | Stronger suction, smarter navigation, longer battery life |
| **Life state path** | Coming home to clean floors, more time with kids, no weekend vacuuming |

**You're anchoring "the liberated life," not "how strong the suction is."**

### Complete Logic of Life State Anchoring

```
Product + Design + Technology → Comprehensive application → Help users achieve certain life state → Mental anchor → Stop comparing competitors
```

**Key elements:**
1. Comprehensive application of product design and technology, not single dimension
2. Describe "life states," not "product features"
3. Users must be able to visualize that picture
4. Once anchored, competitor tech specs become unimportant

### Core Methodology

**Choosing price increase paths:**

| Category | Price Gap | Recommended Path |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| Functional products | Small gap | Tech path |
| Functional products | Large gap | Life state anchoring |
| Emotional products | Any | Story + Identity |

**Key Principles:**
1. When tech gap is small, can take tech path
2. When tech gap is large, must break free from value-for-money thinking
3. Life state anchoring is a deeper strategy than emotional path
4. Anchor to "life state," not "product features"
5. Once successfully anchored, users stop comparing competitors

## Case 10: How Do Skincare Brands Discount Without Damaging Brand?

### Background

An anti-aging cream brand, regular price 300 RMB, wants to offer 20% off (240 RMB) for Double 11 (Singles' Day).

**Concerns:**
- Won't discounting make consumers feel they've been ripped off regularly?
- Won't discounting hurt brand premium?

### Consumer Psychology on Discounting

**Core issue:**
Discounting = Regular prices were rip-offs = Distrust

Consumers think: The discounted price is what the merchant can actually afford; people who bought at regular prices got scammed.

**Therefore, discounting must have legitimate justification.**

### Best Strategy: Clear Inventory Before Product Iteration

**Logic chain:**
```
Product requires update iteration (ingredients/technology upgrade) → Old inventory must clear → Discount is reasonable → Doesn't hurt brand
```

**Messages consumers receive:**
- Not discounting because product is bad
- Normal inventory clearance due to new product launch
- What you're getting is the same as the previous 300 RMB (same ingredients, technology, function)
- You got a deal

### Comparing Two Discounting Approaches

| Discount Method | Consumer Psychology | Brand Impact |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------|
| Unjustified discount | Regularly ripped off | Hurts brand |
| Product iteration clearance | Normal clearance, got a deal | Doesn't hurt brand |

### Execution Points

**Key expressions:**
- "Product about to upgrade, clearance of old version"
- "Same ingredients and technology, better price"
- "Limited inventory, while supplies last"

**What NOT to say:**
- "Promotion," "Special price," "Limited-time offer" (implies regular prices are high)
- Anything that makes consumers think "so the profit margin was this high all along"

### Core Methodology

**Three principles for discounting:**
1. Discounting must have legitimate justification (product iteration is the best reason)
2. Let consumers know what you lost and what you gained
3. You're discounting "old products," not "bad products"

**When discounting is acceptable:**
- Inventory clearance before product iteration ✓
- E-commerce events aligned with product iteration ✓
- New customer acquisition (first-order discount) ✓
- Purely for sales volume ❌ Will hurt brand

## Case 11: Should Internet-Famous Restaurants Expand?

### Background

An internet-famous restaurant with daily lines and great business. Owner wants to open a second location.

**Concern:** Won't expansion dilute brand scarcity?

### Restaurant Specificity: Geographic Origin

Restaurant clientele is geographically origin-based—the geographic range you can cover determines your business success.

### Branch Opening Decision Logic

**Step 1: Is geographic location conflicting?**

| Situation | Geographic Relationship | Impact |
|----------|------------------------|--------|
| Conflicting | Second location within first location's coverage | Divides customers, dilutes brand |
| Non-conflicting | Second location in new geographic area | Covers new customers, doesn't hurt first location |

**Step 2: Are all 3 hard conditions met?**

| Condition | Question | Must be |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Food quality | Can it stay consistent? | ✅ Yes |
| Service experience | Can it be replicated? | ✅ Yes |
| Supply chain | Can it support? | ✅ Yes |

**Any hard condition not met = Branch opening is a false proposition**

### Core Methodology

**Branch opening decision framework:**

```
Geographic location doesn't conflict
    +
Food quality can stay consistent
    +
Service experience can be replicated
    +
Supply chain can support
    ↓
Branch opening is feasible
```

**Key Principles:**
1. Restaurants are geographic-origin businesses; geographic location is the first judgment
2. The latter 3 hard conditions must all be met
3. Any single point not met makes branch opening a false proposition
4. Don't expand for expansion's sake; ensure successful replication

## Case 13: Bottled Tea Pricing (Category Perception & Consumer Appeal)

### Background

A new-style tea brand preparing to launch bottled tea priced at 8 RMB/bottle. Competitors are all in the 5-6 RMB range.

**Owner's concern:** Price too high to sell. Wants to drop to 6 RMB to grab market share.

### Analysis: The Nature of Bottled Tea Category

**Step 1: Understand category consumer appeal**

| Consumer Type | Purchase Motivation | Psychological Price |
|--------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| Tea drinkers | Convenient affordable substitute, seeking basic taste | 5-6 RMB is reasonable |
| Non-tea drinkers | Minimum cost trial | 5-6 RMB is reasonable |

**Both groups are seeking "low-cost tea drink solutions," not "premium experience."**

### Why Is Pricing a Problem?

**8 RMB violates consumer perception in the bottled tea category:**
- It's not a question of "expensive or not"
- It's a question of "reasonable or not"
- Violating consumer perception within a category is a dead end

### Pricing Logic Formula

```
Product positioning → Target audience → Consumer appeal → Price range
```

**Nature of bottled tea:**
- Category = Low-cost tea drink solution
- Audience = People seeking convenient affordable substitutes + People seeking minimum-cost trial
- Appeal = Low cost
- Price range = 5-6 RMB

**Way out for 8 RMB pricing:**
- Break away from the tea drink track
- Transform "tea healthiness" into added value
- Enter from different tracks with main appeal (functional beverages, healthy lifestyle drinks, etc.)

### Core Methodology

**Pricing problems are fundamentally positioning problems:**

| Situation | Core Problem | Solution Direction |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Pricing within category perception | Expensive or not | Lower price or increase value |
| Pricing violating category perception | Reasonable or not | Change category/Change track |

**Key Principles:**
1. Pricing isn't cost accounting; it's matching category perception and consumer appeal
2. Violating consumer perception within a category isn't about "expensive or not"; it's about "reasonable or not"
3. To sell above category perception, must change category

## Case 14: Emotion Water Concept (Social Attributes of Beverage Packaging)

### Background

Beverage market is fiercely competitive. All brands emphasize product quality (taste, ingredients, function).

**Question:** Are there other entry points?

### The True Value of Beverage Packaging Is Being Overlooked

**Packaging essence: Mobile display surface**
- Bottle is a "mobile display"—carrying it around, placing it at work, all convey information to the outside world
- But this "advertising space" only serves merchants, not buyers
- Existing packaging only conveys product information (brand, flavor, ingredients); doesn't help buyers express themselves

### Emotion Water Concept

**Idea: Packaging helps consumers express themselves to the world**

| Traditional Packaging | Emotion Water Packaging |
|----------------------|------------------------|
| Print brand logo, product info | Print emotion labels |
| Conveys "Who I am (product)" | Helps buyer say "How I feel (emotion)" |

**Emotion label examples:**
- "I'm really happy today"
- "I'm a bit annoyed right now"
- "In self-isolation mode"
- "Open to meetups"

**Beverage transforms from "thirst-quenching function" to "emotion expression tool"**

### Why Would Consumers Want This?

**True motivations:**
1. **Social Currency** — Bottle speaks for me, no words needed
2. **Self-expression** — Showing the world my current state
3. **Identity label** — I'm someone who expresses emotions

**Beverage shifts from "product attribute competition" (quality, taste) to "social attribute competition" (helping consumers express themselves)**

### Category Innovation Value

In this new category of "emotion-expressing beverages":
- No price anchor exists
- 8 RMB becomes reasonable
- Consumers aren't buying tea; they're buying "I want others to know my current state"

### Core Methodology

**The true value of packaging isn't displaying products; it's helping buyers express themselves to the world.**

**Category innovation path:**

```
Discover overlooked attributes in existing category → Design new consumption scenarios → Create new category → No price anchor
```

**Key Principles:**
1. Packaging is a "mobile display surface"; its value is severely underestimated
2. Existing packaging only serves merchants, not buyers
3. Helping consumers express themselves is more valuable than displaying product information
4. New categories have no price anchor; greater pricing freedom

## Case 15: Meal Replacement Shake Pricing (Anxiety Relief Category)

### Background

A meal replacement shake brand, cost 15 RMB, wants to price at 39 RMB.

**Market situation:** Competitors are in the 20-50 RMB range, claiming "high value for money."

### Nature of the Meal Replacement Category

**Consumer psychological conflict:**
- Hungry and want to eat → Want to satisfy appetite
- Afraid of regret → Fear of diet failure, weight loss failure

**What meal replacement solves:**
- Eating won't have "punishing consequences"
- Consumers aren't buying meal replacement itself; they're buying "peace of mind that eating won't cause regret"

**True nature of meal replacement category: Anxiety relief**

### Why Competing on Value Is the Worst Strategy?

**Truth about value-for-money consumption:**

The process of consumers selecting products and finding better solutions is itself an enjoyment of achievement. Price is just one dimension for "visualizing self-actualization."

**Competing on value = Letting consumers刷 "money-saving achievement" on you; brand gains nothing.**

### Keys to Winning in Meal Replacement

**Core: Taste + Low calorie + Won't be absorbed**

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| Taste | Satisfies "I want to eat" impulse |
| Low calorie + Won't absorb | Eliminates "eating will cause regret" anxiety (body won't "keep score") |

**For 39 RMB, tell the story of "so delicious you'll forget this is meal replacement," not compete on value.**

### Concept Innovation: Flavor-Calorie Ratio

**Flavor-Calorie Ratio = Taste satisfaction ÷ Calories**

- Higher ratio = More delicious with fewer calories
- Can be quantified, visualized, branded as a differentiator
- This concept doesn't exist in the meal replacement track; whoever says it first owns the perception

### Core Methodology

**Pricing logic for anxiety relief categories:**

```
Identify anxiety source → Design "no-consequence satisfaction" solution → Conceptualize (e.g., flavor-calorie ratio) → Differentiated pricing support
```

**Key Principles:**
1. Meal replacement isn't selling fullness; it's selling "no regret after eating" anxiety relief
2. Competing on value is the worst strategy; brand becomes a tool for consumers to刷 achievement
3. Taste + Low calorie + Won't absorb = Core value closed loop
4. Create quantifiable concepts (like flavor-calorie ratio) to own consumer perception

## Case 16: The Value Trap (Universal Insight)

### Background

Many brands, when competing, first reaction is "compete on value"—cheaper, better deal.

**Question:** Is competing on value really a good strategy?

### Consumer Behavior Has Already Moved Beyond Survival Needs

**Truth of contemporary consumption:**

Consumers are willing to search through vast oceans of products for better value. This behavior itself doesn't mean they can only afford products at this price point.

During this process, they:
1. Enjoy the achievement of "finding better solutions"
2. Price is just one dimension for "visualizing self-actualization"
3. Complete a "I won" psychological experience

### Consequences of Competing on Value

**Brand becomes a tool for consumers to complete "money-saving games":**

| Role | What They Gained |
|------|-----------------|
| Consumer | Achievement of "I saved money, I won" |
| Brand | Nothing |

**Brand is passive in this process; becomes a tool for consumers to刷 achievement; doesn't accumulate any brand value.**

### When Is Competing on Value Acceptable?

| Situation | Appropriate? |
|----------|-------------|
| Early brand building, rapid customer acquisition | Acceptable short-term, not long-term |
| Category itself has no differentiation space | Can only compete on value, thin margins |
| Has differentiated value to mine | Don't compete on value; go value route |
| Want to build long-term brand equity | Absolutely don't compete on value |

### Core Methodology

**Competing on value is the worst strategy:**

```
Competing on value → Consumers刷 achievement on you → Brand accumulates nothing → Thin margins → Can't invest in brand building → More competition
```

**The right direction:**

```
Find differentiated value → Build brand equity → Consumers pay for value → Margins support brand building → Virtuous cycle
```

**Key Principles:**
1. Helping consumers achieve "money-saving achievement" leaves brand with nothing
2. Price is just a tool for consumer self-actualization, not your competitive advantage
3. Unless the category genuinely cannot differentiate, don't proactively compete on value
4. Long-term brand building requires margin support; competing on value creates a vicious cycle
